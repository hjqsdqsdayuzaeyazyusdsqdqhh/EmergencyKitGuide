Home Safety

Home Emergency Checklist

A complete, organized checklist of every supply your household needs to prepare for earthquakes, fires, storms, and power outages. Printable and categorized for easy shopping.

Updated Jul 2025 11 min read By EmergencyKitGuide Editorial Team

Overview

Every year, millions of American households face emergencies that disrupt daily life. According to FEMA, only 39 percent of American families have an emergency plan, and even fewer have a fully stocked emergency kit. Whether you are dealing with a winter storm, summer power outage, earthquake, or house fire, the supplies you have on hand in the first hours after a disaster determine how safe and comfortable your family will be.

This comprehensive home emergency checklist organizes every essential supply into six clear categories. Use it as a shopping guide when building your kit from scratch, or print it out and check off items as you verify what you already have. Each recommendation is based on guidance from FEMA, the American Red Cross, and emergency management professionals.

How to Use This Checklist

Start with Category 1 (Water and Food) and work through each section. At minimum, every household should have the first three categories covered. Categories 4 through 6 add communication tools, comfort items, and specialty supplies that become critical during extended emergencies.

Category 1: Water and Food

Water and food are the foundation of any emergency kit. Without refrigeration or grocery access, your supply must sustain every household member for at least three days.

  • Water (1 gallon per person per day) — Store a minimum three-day supply for every household member. A family of four needs at least 12 gallons. Store commercially bottled water in a cool, dark place away from chemicals.
  • Water purification tablets or filter — If your stored water runs out, purification tablets or a portable filter let you safely drink from rain barrels or other alternative sources.
  • Non-perishable food (3+ days) — Canned goods, peanut butter, crackers, granola bars, dried fruit, nuts, and canned tuna require no cooking or refrigeration. Rotate stock every six months.
  • Manual can opener — Your electric can opener will not work without power. A reliable manual opener is essential for accessing canned food supplies.
  • Cooler with ice packs — Transfer perishable items from your fridge to a cooler immediately when the power goes out. A well-packed cooler keeps food safe for four to six hours.
  • Camping stove or portable burner — A small propane or butane camping stove heats water and cooks food during extended outages. Use it outdoors only for safety. Store extra fuel canisters.
  • Paper plates, cups, and utensils — Without running water, disposable dishware reduces the need for water-intensive cleanup. Stock enough for at least three days.
  • Electrolyte packets — Rehydration salts replace minerals lost through sweating during hot-weather emergencies when air conditioning is unavailable.

Category 2: Lighting and Power

When the power goes out, reliable lighting is your first line of safety. These items keep your home illuminated and your essential devices charged.

  • LED flashlights (2+ per person) — LED flashlights are bright, energy-efficient, and long-lasting. Keep at least two per household member so everyone can move independently during an outage.
  • Headlamps — A headlamp keeps your hands free for cooking, first aid, or caring for children. Essential for navigating dark hallways and stairs safely.
  • Battery-powered lanterns — Lanterns provide ambient room lighting that flashlights cannot. Place one in each main living area for family comfort during extended darkness.
  • Extra batteries (all sizes) — Stock AA, AAA, C, D, and 9V batteries. Store them in a cool, dry location in their original packaging. Check expiration dates every six months.
  • Portable power bank (20,000mAh+) — A high-capacity power bank keeps your phone charged for communication and emergency alerts. Charge it fully before storm season.
  • Car phone charger — If your home power is out but your car runs, you can charge devices in your vehicle. Keep a dual-port USB charger in your glove box.
  • Solar-powered charger — A foldable solar panel charges power banks and phones using sunlight. Essential for outages lasting more than a few days.
  • Candles and windproof matches — Candles provide soft light but pose fire risks. Keep them on stable surfaces away from curtains and never leave them unattended.

Category 3: Safety and First Aid

Home emergencies increase the risk of injuries from falls, fires, and carbon monoxide exposure. These items protect your family from common household hazards.

  • First aid kit — Include adhesive bandages, gauze pads, medical tape, antiseptic wipes, antibiotic ointment, pain relievers, tweezers, and disposable gloves. Customize for your family's specific needs.
  • Fire extinguisher (ABC-rated) — A small ABC-rated fire extinguisher is critical during kitchen fires, electrical fires, and candle-related incidents. Keep one on each floor.
  • Carbon monoxide detector (battery-powered) — CO detectors with battery backup continue protecting you when plugged-in models lose power. Place one near bedrooms.
  • Smoke detectors (battery-powered) — Ensure battery-powered smoke detectors are installed on every level and in every bedroom. Test them monthly.
  • Whistle — A loud whistle can signal for help if you are trapped under debris or need to alert neighbors during an emergency.
  • Dust masks (N95 or P100) — After an earthquake or building collapse, dust and debris fill the air. N95 masks protect your lungs from harmful particles.
  • Work gloves — Protect your hands from broken glass, debris, and sharp edges during cleanup after a storm or earthquake.
  • Sturdy shoes near your bed — Broken glass and debris are common hazards during earthquakes and storms. Keep closed-toe shoes within reach at night.

Category 4: Warmth and Shelter

Without heating in winter or air conditioning in summer, indoor temperatures can become dangerous within hours. These items help maintain a safe body temperature.

  • Mylar emergency blankets — Mylar space blankets reflect up to 90% of body heat and weigh almost nothing. Keep several for each family member.
  • Wool or fleece blankets — Heavier blankets provide much more warmth than mylar alone. Store at least one per person in your emergency supply area.
  • Sleeping bags — Rated sleeping bags keep you warm even in unheated rooms. Choose a bag rated 20 degrees below your region's typical winter low.
  • Hand and body warmers — Disposable hand warmers generate heat for 8 to 12 hours. Place them inside gloves, hats, or sleeping bags during cold-weather emergencies.
  • Extra warm clothing layers — Layer thermal underwear, fleece jackets, wool socks, hats, and gloves. Wear layers indoors when heating is unavailable.
  • Plastic sheeting and tape — Cover broken windows and seal drafts to retain heat or keep out smoke. Heavy-duty plastic sheeting and duct tape create an effective temporary barrier.
  • Battery-powered fan — During summer emergencies, a battery-powered fan provides airflow and helps prevent heat-related illness when air conditioning is down.
  • Insulated window coverings — Reflective window insulation or thick curtains reduce heat loss in winter and block solar heat gain in summer.

Pro Tip

During a winter emergency, gather your family in one room and close the door. Use blankets and towels to seal the door frame. Body heat from multiple people in a small space can raise the temperature significantly. Cover windows with blankets and huddle together under sleeping bags.

Category 5: Communication and Documents

Staying informed and having access to critical documents during an emergency is vital. These items ensure you receive updates and can prove identity or insurance coverage.

  • NOAA weather radio — A battery-powered or hand-crank weather radio receives emergency broadcasts from the National Weather Service when cell networks and internet are down.
  • Portable phone charger — Keep your primary phone charged to receive emergency alerts, contact family, and access GPS. Use power-saving mode to extend battery life.
  • Emergency contact list (printed) — Write key phone numbers on paper. Do not rely solely on your phone's contact list, as your phone may die.
  • Important documents in waterproof bag — Store copies of IDs, insurance policies, medical records, and bank information in a waterproof, fireproof container.
  • Cash in small bills — ATMs and card readers require electricity. Keep at least $50 to $100 in small bills for emergency purchases.
  • Multi-tool or basic tool kit — A multi-tool with pliers, screwdrivers, and a knife handles many repair tasks. Add wrenches and screwdrivers for utility shut-off valves.
  • Paper map of your area — GPS and data services may be unavailable. A paper map helps you navigate to shelters, hospitals, or supply points.
  • Duct tape and zip ties — Invaluable for temporary repairs, sealing leaks, and securing items during and after an emergency.

Category 6: Comfort and Specialty Items

These items are easy to overlook but make a significant difference in comfort and well-being during extended emergencies, especially with children, infants, or elderly family members.

  • Baby supplies — If you have an infant, stock formula, bottles, diapers, wipes, and a change of clothing. Pack enough for at least five days.
  • Pet food and supplies — Store at least three days of pet food, water bowls, a leash, waste bags, and any medications your pet needs.
  • Entertainment for children — Card games, coloring books, puzzles, and small toys help keep children calm and occupied during stressful emergencies.
  • Feminine hygiene products — Stock pads, tampons, or menstrual cups along with pain relievers. These are essential and often forgotten in emergency kits.
  • Prescription medications — Keep a 7-day supply of all prescription medications in a waterproof container. Rotate stock before expiration dates.
  • Glasses or contact lenses — Keep an extra pair of prescription glasses or contact lenses with solution in your emergency kit.
  • Sanitation supplies — Garbage bags, moist towelettes, hand sanitizer, and a small shovel for waste disposal maintain hygiene when plumbing is disrupted.
  • Comfort items — A favorite blanket, pillow, or small personal item can provide emotional comfort during a stressful emergency, especially for children.

Home Emergency Kit Maintenance

An emergency kit is only useful if everything inside it works when you need it. Follow these maintenance steps to keep your kit ready year-round.

  • Check water supply every six months — Replace stored water and check bottled water expiration dates. Use a calendar reminder tied to daylight saving time changes.
  • Rotate food stock — Use and replace non-perishable items before their expiration dates. Follow the "first in, first out" method to keep supplies fresh.
  • Test all electronics — Power on flashlights, radios, and lanterns every six months. Replace batteries that have weakened. Recharge all power banks fully.
  • Update medications — Replace expired prescription and over-the-counter medications. Check that dosages are still appropriate for family members.
  • Review clothing seasonally — Swap out seasonal clothing in your kit. In spring, replace heavy winter gear with rain gear and sun protection. In fall, reverse the process.
  • Inspect first aid supplies — Replace used or expired bandages, antiseptic wipes, and medications. Ensure your kit has supplies for any new family members or changing medical needs.
  • Check smoke and CO detectors — Test all smoke and carbon monoxide detectors monthly. Replace batteries annually and replace the entire unit every 10 years.
  • Review and practice your plan — Walk through your emergency plan with your family twice a year. Identify meeting points, practice evacuation routes, and review emergency contacts.

Home Emergency Checklist FAQs

Every household should have at least three kits: a 72-hour go-bag by the front door, a shelter-in-place supply kit in a central location, and a smaller car kit in your vehicle. Families with infants, pets, or elderly members should add specialty kits tailored to those needs.
A basic home emergency kit includes water (one gallon per person per day for three days), non-perishable food, flashlights with extra batteries, a first aid kit, important documents in a waterproof bag, a whistle, dust masks, plastic sheeting, a multi-tool, and a battery-powered or hand-crank radio.
Review and restock your home emergency kit every six months. Replace expired food and water, check battery charge levels, update medications, and adjust clothing seasonally. Set a recurring calendar reminder for April and October to align with daylight saving time changes.
A generator is recommended but not required. If you purchase one, use it outdoors only, at least 20 feet from windows and doors, to prevent carbon monoxide poisoning. A portable power bank or solar charger is a safer alternative for keeping phones and small devices charged.
Store commercially bottled water in a cool, dark place away from direct sunlight and chemicals. Use food-grade water storage containers if filling your own. Replace stored water every six months. For larger supplies, consider 55-gallon water barrels with a hand pump for dispensing.
