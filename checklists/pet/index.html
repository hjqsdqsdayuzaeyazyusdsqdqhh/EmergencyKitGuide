Pet Safety

Pet Emergency Checklist

A complete, organized checklist of every supply your dog, cat, or other pet needs during an emergency. Covers food, water, medication, evacuation, and identification.

Updated Jul 2025 10 min read By EmergencyKitGuide Editorial Team

Overview

Approximately 70 percent of American households own at least one pet, yet studies show that fewer than half of pet owners have an emergency plan that includes their animals. When disaster strikes, pets are among the most vulnerable members of your household. They depend entirely on you for food, water, shelter, and medical care, and they cannot communicate their needs during a crisis.

This pet emergency checklist covers every supply and planning step needed to keep your pet safe during natural disasters, power outages, house fires, and extended emergencies. Each recommendation is based on guidance from the American Veterinary Medical Association (AVMA), FEMA, and the Red Cross.

How to Use This Checklist

Start with Categories 1 and 2, which cover the essentials every pet needs. Then add items from the remaining categories based on your pet type and any special medical or behavioral needs. Store all pet emergency supplies in a clearly labeled, waterproof container near your main emergency kit.

Category 1: Food and Water

Pets need consistent access to food and water just like humans. During emergencies, grocery stores and supply chains may be disrupted for days or weeks.

  • Pet food (7-day supply) — Store at least seven days of dry or canned food for each pet. Canned food provides moisture, which is beneficial if water is limited. Rotate stock every three to six months.
  • Water for pets — Store at least seven days of water specifically for your pets. Dogs and cats typically need about one ounce of water per pound of body weight per day.
  • Collapsible food and water bowls — Lightweight, portable bowls are easy to pack in a go-bag and essential during evacuations.
  • Manual can opener — If you store canned pet food, you need a manual opener since electric openers will not work without power.
  • Pet-safe water purification — If your tap water is contaminated, a pet-safe water filter or purification tablets ensure your pet stays hydrated safely.
  • High-calorie treats — High-value treats help keep pets calm during stressful situations and can be used for training or distraction.
  • Feeding schedule card — Write down your pet feeding schedule, portion sizes, and any dietary restrictions. A pet sitter or shelter volunteer may need this information.
  • Salt lick for dogs — Electrolyte replacement helps dogs recover from heat stress during summer emergencies when air conditioning is unavailable.

Category 2: Carrier, Leash, and Restraint

Proper restraint keeps your pet safe during evacuations and prevents escape in unfamiliar or chaotic environments.

  • Carrier or crate for each pet — A sturdy, well-ventilated carrier is essential for cats, small dogs, and exotic pets. Label each carrier with your pet name, your name, and your phone number.
  • Leash and collar with ID tags — A standard six-foot leash is required at most shelters. Ensure your pet collar has current ID tags with your phone number and an out-of-state contact.
  • Harness for dogs — A harness provides better control than a collar alone, especially for large or anxious dogs during evacuations.
  • Extra leash and collar — Pack a backup leash and collar in case the primary one breaks or gets lost during an emergency.
  • Pet muzzle — Even gentle pets may bite when frightened or injured. A soft muzzle keeps your pet and rescue workers safe during first aid or evacuation.
  • Long tie-out cable — A 20 to 30-foot cable with a stake allows you to secure your dog outdoors at a shelter, park, or temporary camp.
  • Carrier bedding — Place a familiar blanket or towel in each carrier to reduce stress. The scent of home provides comfort in unfamiliar environments.
  • Car safety harness or crate — Secure your pet in the vehicle during evacuation. An unrestrained pet can be injured in sudden stops or distract the driver.

Category 3: Medical and Records

Medical supplies and records are critical if your pet needs veterinary care during or after an emergency.

  • Prescription medications (14-day supply) — Maintain a two-week supply of all prescription medications your pet takes. Ask your vet for an extra prescription specifically for emergencies.
  • Pet first aid kit — Include gauze, adhesive tape, antiseptic wipes, tweezers, scissors, styptic powder, hydrogen peroxide (to induce vomiting only under vet guidance), and a pet first aid guide.
  • Vaccination records — Keep copies of your pet current vaccinations in a waterproof bag. Many shelters require proof of rabies vaccination.
  • Medical history and vet contact — Print your pet medical history and your veterinarian contact information. Include any allergy or condition information.
  • Flea and tick prevention — Disasters can increase exposure to parasites. Pack a supply of flea and tick prevention medication.
  • Heartworm medication — Do not miss doses during extended emergencies. Pack a monthly supply of heartworm prevention.
  • Microchip registration information — Keep your microchip registration number and the company contact information in your go-bag. Verify your contact information is current in the database.
  • Recent photos of your pet — Print two to three recent color photos of each pet. If your pet gets lost, photos are essential for identification on lost pet websites and shelter postings.

Category 4: Sanitation and Hygiene

Maintaining hygiene prevents illness and keeps your pet comfortable during extended emergencies.

  • Waste bags — Pack at least 50 waste bags for dogs. Proper waste disposal prevents contamination at shelters and campsites.
  • Cat litter and portable litter box — A small, disposable litter box and a bag of litter are essential for cat owners during evacuations or shelter-in-place situations.
  • Pet-safe disinfectant — Clean crates, carriers, and bowls with a pet-safe disinfectant to prevent the spread of disease.
  • Paper towels and cleaning cloths — For cleaning up messes in carriers, cars, or temporary shelters.
  • Pet shampoo (unscented) — If your pet gets exposed to floodwater, chemicals, or debris, a gentle shampoo removes contaminants safely.
  • Grooming supplies — A brush, nail clippers, and ear cleaning solution maintain your pet health during extended emergencies.
  • Poop scoop or trowel — A small garden trowel or dedicated poop scoop makes waste cleanup easier at campsites and temporary housing.
  • Garbage bags for waste disposal — Sealable garbage bags contain odors and waste at shelters and temporary living spaces.

Category 5: Evacuation Planning

Evacuating with a pet requires advance planning. Do not wait until an emergency to figure out where you and your pet will go.

  • Pet-friendly hotel list — Research and list at least five pet-friendly hotels along your evacuation route. Many hotels relax pet policies during declared emergencies.
  • Animal shelter and boarding contacts — Identify local animal shelters, boarding facilities, and veterinary clinics that accept pets during emergencies.
  • Friends and family with pet-friendly homes — Identify at least two people outside your area who can take you and your pet in during an evacuation.
  • Evacuation route map — Mark pet-friendly stops along your primary and secondary evacuation routes on a paper map.
  • Vehicle pet preparation — Ensure your vehicle can safely transport your pet. Keep a pet seat cover, water bowl, and portable crate in your car at all times.
  • Pet go-bag near the door — Store your pet emergency kit near your front door alongside your family go-bags for quick departure.
  • Emergency contact card for pet — Attach a card to your pet collar with your name, phone number, out-of-state contact, and any medical information.
  • Practice evacuation with your pet — Practice loading your pet into the car, securing them in a crate, and driving to a meeting point. Familiarity reduces stress during real emergencies.

Pro Tip

Never leave your pet behind during an evacuation. If you cannot take your pet, leave them in a secure room with food, water, and a sign on the door indicating a pet is inside. Contact local animal rescue organizations for assistance.

Category 6: Special Needs Pets

Senior pets, pets with medical conditions, and exotic animals all require additional planning and supplies.

  • Senior pet supplements — Joint supplements, senior-specific food, and extra bedding for older pets with mobility issues.
  • Prescription diet food — If your pet is on a therapeutic diet, stock extra supplies. These specialized foods are harder to find during emergencies.
  • Exotic pet supplies — Reptiles need heat sources and specific substrate. Birds need seed, grit, and a covered carrier. Research your exotic pet specific needs.
  • Anxiety aids — Thunder shirts, calming pheromone sprays, or anxiety wraps help stressed pets cope with loud noises and unfamiliar environments.
  • Portable water filter for large dogs — Large dogs consume significant water. A portable filter lets you safely refill from alternative water sources.
  • Wheelchair or mobility aid — If your pet uses a wheelchair or sling, pack a backup or repair kit for the device.
  • Insulated pet coat or booties — Senior and short-haired pets need extra warmth during cold-weather emergencies. Waterproof booties protect paws from debris and hot surfaces.
  • Pet GPS tracker — A GPS tracker on your pet collar helps locate them quickly if they escape during an emergency.

After the Emergency: Pet Recovery Tips

Once the immediate danger has passed, take these steps to help your pet recover and return to normal.

  • Check for injuries — Examine your pet for cuts, burns, or signs of distress. Contact your veterinarian if you notice anything unusual.
  • Re-establish routine — Return to regular feeding, walking, and sleeping schedules as quickly as possible. Routine provides comfort and reduces anxiety.
  • Monitor behavior changes — Watch for signs of stress such as excessive barking, hiding, loss of appetite, or aggression. Consult your vet if behavioral changes persist.
  • Restock your pet emergency kit — Replace any used items immediately. You never know when the next emergency will occur.
  • Update ID and microchip — If you have moved or changed phone numbers during the emergency, update your pet ID tags and microchip registration immediately.
  • Check for hazards — Inspect your home and yard for debris, standing water, or chemicals that could harm your pet before letting them roam freely.
