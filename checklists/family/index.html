Family Safety

Family Emergency Checklist

A complete, family-focused emergency checklist covering supplies, communication plans, and safety gear for every member of your household, including babies, children, and seniors.

Updated Jul 2025 12 min read By EmergencyKitGuide Editorial Team

Overview

Families face unique challenges during emergencies. According to the Red Cross, only 1 in 4 American families has a practiced emergency plan, and most households lack adequate supplies for even a three-day disruption. Children, infants, seniors, and family members with medical needs all require specific items that generic emergency checklists often overlook.

This family emergency checklist goes beyond the basics. It covers the essential supplies every household needs, plus specialty items for babies, children, elderly family members, and pets. It also includes a family communication plan template so every household member knows what to do when disaster strikes.

How to Use This Checklist

Start with the core family supplies in Categories 1 through 4, then move to the family-specific sections that apply to your household. If you have infants, children, seniors, or pets, complete those additional categories. Print this checklist and review it as a family so everyone knows where supplies are stored.

Category 1: Core Family Supplies

These are the non-negotiable supplies every family needs, regardless of the type of emergency. Build your family's kit around these essentials first.

  • Water (1 gallon per person per day) — Store a minimum three-day supply for every household member. A family of four needs at least 12 gallons. For a week-long emergency, you will need 28 gallons for four people.
  • Non-perishable food (3+ days) — Canned goods, peanut butter, crackers, granola bars, dried fruit, and nuts require no cooking. Include a manual can opener and disposable plates and utensils.
  • Flashlights and lanterns — LED flashlights for each family member plus battery-powered lanterns for shared areas. Stock extra batteries in all sizes.
  • First aid kit — Include bandages, antiseptic wipes, antibiotic ointment, pain relievers, allergy medication, and any prescription medications your family takes daily.
  • Emergency blankets and sleeping bags — Mylar blankets for each family member plus wool or fleece blankets for warmth. Sleeping bags rated for your region's lowest temperatures.
  • Important documents (waterproof bag) — Copies of IDs, birth certificates, insurance policies, medical records, and bank information stored in a waterproof, fireproof container.
  • Cash in small bills — ATMs and card readers require electricity. Keep at least $100 in small bills for emergency purchases.
  • Multi-tool and duct tape — A multi-tool with pliers, screwdrivers, and a knife handles many repair tasks. Duct tape and zip ties are invaluable for temporary fixes.

Category 2: Family Communication Plan

When disaster strikes, cell networks may be overloaded and internet access may be unavailable. A pre-established communication plan ensures every family member knows what to do and who to contact.

  • Emergency contact cards — Create a card for each family member with two emergency contacts: one local and one out-of-state. Include phone numbers, addresses, and meeting points.
  • Out-of-state contact person — Choose a friend or relative who lives in another state as your family's central communication point. Local calls may be blocked, but long-distance calls often work.
  • Two meeting points — Identify a meeting spot near your home (a mailbox or neighbor's house) and one outside your neighborhood (a relative's house or community center).
  • Printed phone numbers — Write key numbers on paper. Do not rely solely on your phone's contact list. Include schools, doctors, pharmacies, and workplaces.
  • NOAA weather radio — A battery-powered or hand-crank radio receives emergency broadcasts when cell networks and internet are down.
  • Whistle for each family member — A loud whistle lets family members signal each other during evacuations or in dark environments.
  • Local area map — A paper map of your city with evacuation routes marked. GPS and data services may be unavailable during emergencies.
  • Family meeting practice — Practice your evacuation plan and meeting points with your family twice a year. Ensure children can recite emergency phone numbers.

Pro Tip

Teach every family member to text instead of call during an emergency. Texts use less bandwidth and are more likely to go through when cell networks are congested. Practice sending a simple "I am safe" text to your out-of-state contact.

Category 3: Shelter-in-Place Supplies

When staying home during an emergency, these supplies keep your family safe and comfortable until the situation resolves.

  • Plastic sheeting and duct tape — Seal doors, windows, and vents during chemical or biological emergencies. Create a safe room by sealing one interior room.
  • Dust masks (N95 or P100) — Protect lungs from dust, smoke, and airborne debris. Keep masks sized for both adults and children.
  • Fire extinguisher (ABC-rated) — Keep one on each floor. Teach older children how to use one safely.
  • Battery-powered CO and smoke detectors — Battery backup ensures detectors work during power outages. Place one near every bedroom.
  • Work gloves for each adult — Protect hands from broken glass, debris, and sharp edges during cleanup.
  • Sturdy shoes near each bed — Broken glass and debris are common after earthquakes and storms. Keep closed-toe shoes within reach at night.
  • Sanitation supplies — Garbage bags, moist towelettes, hand sanitizer, and a small shovel for waste disposal when plumbing is disrupted.
  • Baby and pet supplies — Formula, diapers, pet food, and a leash stored in your emergency area for quick access.

Category 4: Evacuation Go-Bag

When you need to leave quickly, each family member should have a packed go-bag ready. Pre-pack these bags and store them near your front door or in an accessible closet.

  • Water (1 liter per person) — A small bottle of water per person for immediate hydration during evacuation.
  • Energy bars or trail mix — Compact, high-calorie snacks that require no preparation.
  • Change of clothing — One full change of clothes per family member, including socks and underwear. Dress in layers for temperature changes.
  • Rain poncho — Lightweight and compact. Keeps you dry during wet evacuations.
  • Small backpack per family member — Each person carries their own essentials. Children's bags should be lightweight and age-appropriate.
  • Flashlight or headlamp — A small personal flashlight for each go-bag.
  • Portable phone charger — A fully charged power bank for each go-bag.
  • Cash and copies of IDs — Small bills and photocopies of important documents in a zip-lock bag.

Category 5: Baby and Infant Supplies

Infants require specific supplies that are easy to forget in an emergency kit. Stock these items in a separate, clearly labeled container for quick access.

  • Formula or baby food (5-day supply) — Pack enough for at least five days. Include bottles, nipples, and a bottle brush for cleaning.
  • Diapers and wipes — Pack at least 50 diapers and multiple packs of wipes. Newborns can use 10 to 12 diapers per day.
  • Baby clothing and blankets — Pack several onesies, sleep sacks, and a warm blanket. Dress babies in layers for temperature changes.
  • Pacifiers and comfort items — Pack extra pacifiers and a favorite stuffed animal or blanket to reduce stress.
  • Diaper rash cream and baby-safe soap — Maintain hygiene during extended emergencies with gentle, baby-safe products.
  • Baby medications — Infant pain reliever, gas drops, and any prescribed medications. Check expiration dates every six months.
  • Car seat — Ensure your car seat is properly installed and up to date. Keep the registration card in your go-bag.
  • Baby carrier or sling — A hands-free carrier keeps your baby secure during evacuations when a stroller is impractical.

Category 6: Children's Supplies

Children have unique emotional and physical needs during emergencies. These items keep them safe, comfortable, and calm.

  • Extra clothing and shoes — Pack a full change of clothes and sturdy shoes for each child. Include weather-appropriate layers.
  • Children's medications — Pain relievers, allergy medications, and any prescribed medications in child-appropriate doses.
  • Comfort items — A favorite toy, stuffed animal, book, or blanket can provide emotional security during a stressful emergency.
  • Entertainment — Card games, coloring books, crayons, small puzzles, and age-appropriate activities help keep children calm and occupied.
  • Dust masks (child-sized) — Standard adult masks do not fit children properly. Keep child-sized N95 masks in your kit.
  • Identification for each child — Write each child's name, your phone number, and an out-of-state contact on a card and place it in their pocket or backpack.
  • Water bottle — A reusable water bottle for each child encourages hydration and reduces plastic waste.
  • Sunscreen and insect repellent — Protect children from sun and insect exposure during outdoor evacuations or extended power outages.

Category 7: Senior and Medical Needs

Older adults and family members with medical conditions require additional planning and supplies. Address these needs before an emergency occurs.

  • Prescription medications (7-day supply) — Maintain a rolling seven-day supply of all prescription medications. Keep a printed list of medications, dosages, and prescribing doctors.
  • Medical equipment backups — If a family member uses a CPAP machine, glucose monitor, or other medical device, keep batteries and backup supplies on hand.
  • Glasses and hearing aid batteries — Extra prescription glasses and a supply of hearing aid batteries prevent loss of critical sensory aids.
  • Medical identification — Ensure seniors and family members with medical conditions wear medical ID bracelets or carry ID cards listing conditions and medications.
  • Mobility aids — Keep a backup cane, wheelchair, or walker accessible. Store a lightweight folding chair for rest during evacuations.
  • Incontinence supplies — Pack adult incontinence products and disposable wipes for dignity and hygiene during extended emergencies.
  • Non-perishable dietary foods — Stock foods appropriate for dietary restrictions: low-sodium, diabetic-friendly, or soft foods for those with difficulty chewing.
  • Emergency contact information — A card listing all doctors, pharmacies, insurance information, and emergency contacts for each family member with medical needs.

How to Practice Your Family Emergency Plan

An emergency plan only works if every family member knows it by heart. Follow these steps to practice and refine your plan.

  • Conduct a family meeting — Sit down with everyone and explain the emergency plan in age-appropriate language. Answer questions and address fears honestly.
  • Practice evacuation twice a year — Set a timer and have everyone grab their go-bags and meet at the designated meeting point. Time the drill and try to improve.
  • Teach children to call 911 — Practice reciting your home address and a parent's phone number. Role-play calling 911 so children know what to say.
  • Review meeting points — Drive to your neighborhood meeting point and outside meeting point so every family member recognizes them.
  • Check emergency supplies — During each drill, open your emergency kit and verify everything is current. Replace expired items and update seasonal clothing.
  • Practice fire escape routes — Identify two exits from every room. Practice crawling low under smoke and feeling doors for heat before opening.
  • Update contact cards — Verify phone numbers and addresses on emergency contact cards every six months. Replace cards for children who have outgrown them.
  • Discuss different scenarios — Talk through what to do in different emergencies: fire, earthquake, tornado, flood, and power outage. Each situation has a different response.
