Travel Safety

Travel Emergency Checklist

A complete, organized checklist of every supply you need for road trips, flights, and vacation travel. Stay safe and prepared wherever your journey takes you.

Updated Jul 2025 11 min read By EmergencyKitGuide Editorial Team

Why Every Traveler Needs an Emergency Kit

The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration reports over 6 million car accidents annually in the United States. Beyond car crashes, travelers face risks from severe weather, medical emergencies, flight delays, and natural disasters in unfamiliar locations. Having the right supplies on hand can mean the difference between a minor inconvenience and a life-threatening situation.

This travel emergency checklist covers every essential supply for road trips, flights, and vacation travel. Each category is designed to be packed in a compact, grab-and-go format that fits in your trunk, carry-on, or hotel room. Use it as a packing guide before every trip.

How to Use This Checklist

Start with Categories 1 and 2, which cover the vehicle and first aid essentials. Then add items from the remaining categories based on your destination, mode of travel, and length of trip. Pack your travel emergency kit in a dedicated bag that stays in your vehicle or luggage.

Category 1: Vehicle and Roadside Essentials

For road trips, your vehicle is your lifeline. These items keep you safe and mobile if you break down or get stranded.

  • Spare tire, jack, and lug wrench — Verify your spare is properly inflated before every trip. Practice changing a tire at home so you are prepared on the road.
  • Jumper cables or portable jump starter — A portable jump starter is compact and does not require another vehicle. Keep it fully charged.
  • Roadside flares or reflective triangles — Place behind your vehicle to warn oncoming traffic during breakdowns. Three triangles placed 50 feet apart provide maximum visibility.
  • Basic tool kit — Include a multi-tool, screwdrivers, pliers, duct tape, and zip ties for temporary repairs.
  • Motor oil and coolant — A quart of oil and a gallon of coolant can prevent engine damage if you notice leaks or overheating.
  • Windshield washer fluid — Essential for maintaining visibility in rain, snow, and dusty conditions.
  • Tire pressure gauge — Check tire pressure before and during long trips. Underinflated tires cause blowouts and reduce fuel efficiency.
  • Tow strap or rope — A rated tow strap can pull your vehicle out of a ditch or snowbank with the help of another vehicle.

Category 2: First Aid and Health

Medical emergencies do not wait for you to get home. A well-stocked travel first aid kit handles minor injuries and stabilizes more serious ones until help arrives.

  • Compact first aid kit — Include adhesive bandages, gauze, medical tape, antiseptic wipes, antibiotic ointment, tweezers, scissors, and disposable gloves.
  • Pain relievers and anti-inflammatory medication — Ibuprofen, acetaminophen, and aspirin cover headaches, muscle pain, and fever.
  • Motion sickness medication — Essential for passengers prone to car or air sickness. Pack both pills and wristbands.
  • Prescription medications (7-day supply) — Carry more than you need in case of travel delays. Keep medications in original containers.
  • Sunscreen and insect repellent — SPF 30+ sunscreen and DEET-based repellent protect against sunburn and insect-borne diseases.
  • Hand sanitizer and wet wipes — Maintain hygiene when soap and water are not available at rest stops or airports.
  • Electrolyte packets — Rehydration salts replace minerals lost during long drives, hikes, or hot-weather travel.
  • Emergency prescription info card — A card listing your medications, dosages, allergies, and prescribing doctor for emergencies.

Category 3: Food and Water

Being stranded without food or water is dangerous, especially in remote areas. Stock compact, non-perishable supplies for every trip.

  • Water bottles (1 liter per person) — Store sealed water bottles in your vehicle. Replace every six months or after extreme heat.
  • Energy bars and trail mix — High-calorie, compact snacks that require no preparation. Pack enough for 24 hours of unexpected delays.
  • Canned food and manual opener — Canned tuna, beans, or fruit provide nutrition during extended delays. Do not forget the manual can opener.
  • Collapsible water container — A foldable water container lets you carry water from gas stations or rest stops to your vehicle.
  • Paper cups and utensils — Disposable dishware for eating without access to a kitchen or running water.
  • Insulated cooler — A small cooler keeps perishable snacks and drinks cold during long drives. Use ice packs instead of loose ice.
  • Water purification tablets — If you run out of bottled water in a remote area, purification tablets let you safely drink from alternative sources.
  • High-energy emergency rations — 3600-calorie emergency food bars have a five-year shelf life and provide nutrition for up to three days.

Category 4: Navigation and Communication

Getting lost or losing communication in an unfamiliar area is a common travel emergency. These items keep you connected and oriented.

  • Offline maps (downloaded) — Download Google Maps or Maps.me offline maps for your entire route before departing. Cell service may be unavailable in rural areas.
  • Paper road map or atlas — GPS and phones fail. A paper map of your region is a reliable backup that works without batteries.
  • Portable phone charger (20,000mAh+) — A high-capacity power bank keeps your phone charged for communication and GPS during extended delays.
  • Car phone charger — A dual-port USB charger keeps devices powered while driving. Keep one in your glove box at all times.
  • NOAA weather radio (compact) — A small weather radio receives severe weather alerts when cell networks are overloaded or unavailable.
  • Whistle — A loud whistle can signal for help if you are stranded in a remote area. Attach one to your keychain.
  • Printed emergency contact list — Write key phone numbers on paper. Include roadside assistance, insurance, and an out-of-state contact.
  • Solar-powered charger — A foldable solar panel charges devices during extended outdoor emergencies or multi-day road trips.

Pro Tip

Before every road trip, text your itinerary to a trusted friend or family member. Include your route, expected arrival times, and hotel information. If you do not check in at the expected time, they will know where to send help.

Category 5: Documents and Money

Lost or stolen documents can turn a vacation into a crisis. Protect yourself with backups and accessible cash.

  • Driver license and ID — Carry your primary ID at all times. Keep a photocopy stored separately in your luggage.
  • Passport (international travel) — Keep your passport in a waterproof, RFID-blocking travel wallet. Store a photocopy in a different bag.
  • Insurance cards — Health insurance, travel insurance, and vehicle insurance cards. Take photos of each card and store them in cloud storage.
  • Vehicle registration and proof of insurance — Keep these in your glove box. Digital copies on your phone are a useful backup.
  • Cash in small bills ($100-$200) — ATMs and card readers may be unavailable in remote areas. Keep cash in your wallet, glove box, and a hidden luggage spot.
  • Credit and debit cards — Carry at least two cards from different banks in case one is declined or lost.
  • Hotel and reservation confirmations — Print confirmations or save screenshots. Do not rely solely on email access.
  • Emergency contact card — A card with your name, phone number, blood type, allergies, and emergency contacts. Keep in your wallet.

Category 6: Clothing and Shelter

Weather changes and unexpected overnight delays require adaptable clothing and shelter supplies.

  • Rain poncho — Lightweight and compact. Keeps you dry during roadside repairs or outdoor emergencies.
  • Emergency mylar blanket — Reflects body heat and weighs almost nothing. Essential for cold-weather breakdowns.
  • Extra clothing layer — Pack a fleece jacket, wool socks, and a hat even in summer. Mountain and desert temperatures drop dramatically at night.
  • Sturdy closed-toe shoes — Keep a pair of shoes in your vehicle for walking after a breakdown. Sandals and heels are not suitable for roadside emergencies.
  • Work gloves — Protect your hands during tire changes, engine repairs, or clearing debris.
  • Sunglasses and hat — Protect against sun glare during roadside repairs and outdoor emergencies.
  • Compact sleeping bag or bivy — For overnight stranding in remote areas. A lightweight bivy sack packs small and provides warmth.
  • Travel umbrella — A compact umbrella keeps you dry while loading luggage or walking to your destination in rain.

Common Travel Emergency Preparedness Mistakes

Avoid these frequent mistakes that leave travelers vulnerable during emergencies.

  • Driving with an empty gas tank — Never let your tank drop below a quarter full, especially in rural areas. Gas stations may be far apart.
  • Ignoring tire condition — Worn tires blow out more frequently. Check tread depth and pressure before every long trip.
  • Not downloading offline maps — Cell service is unreliable in mountains, deserts, and rural areas. Always download maps before departing.
  • Traveling without cash — Card readers fail during power outages. Small bills buy gas, food, and supplies when electronic payments are unavailable.
  • Skipping vehicle maintenance — Oil changes, brake inspections, and fluid checks prevent breakdowns. Service your vehicle before long trips.
  • Forgetting medications — Pack a full supply of prescription medications plus a few extra days in case of travel delays.

Travel Kit Maintenance Schedule

Your travel emergency kit needs regular maintenance to ensure everything works when you need it.

  • Before every trip — Check that your kit is packed and complete. Verify water, snacks, and medications are current. Test your flashlight and charger.
  • Every 6 months — Replace expired food and water. Update printed documents and contact lists. Check battery levels in all electronics.
  • Seasonally — Swap clothing and supplies for the season. Add warm layers in fall and sun protection in spring.
  • After every trip — Restock any used items. Clean and dry reusable supplies. Update your kit based on what you used or wished you had.
