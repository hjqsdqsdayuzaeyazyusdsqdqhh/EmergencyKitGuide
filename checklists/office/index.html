Workplace Safety

Office Emergency Checklist

A complete, organized checklist of every supply your workplace needs for fires, evacuations, medical emergencies, power outages, and severe weather. Printable and categorized.

Updated Jul 2025 11 min read By EmergencyKitGuide Editorial Team

Why Office Emergency Preparedness Matters

Workplace emergencies happen without warning. According to the National Safety Council, a worker is injured on the job every seven seconds, and the Bureau of Labor Statistics reports over 5,000 workplace fatalities annually. Fires, severe weather, medical emergencies, and active shooter situations all require immediate action and the right supplies on hand.

OSHA requires employers to maintain basic emergency preparedness, but many offices fall short. This checklist covers every essential supply your workplace needs, organized into seven clear categories. Use it as a shopping guide, a compliance audit, or a printable reference for your office emergency plan.

How to Use This Checklist

Start with Categories 1 and 2, which cover the legal requirements every office must meet. Then work through the remaining categories based on your office size, number of employees, and local hazard profile. Review this checklist with your safety officer or office manager.

Category 1: Fire Safety and Evacuation

Fire is the most common workplace emergency. OSHA requires specific fire safety equipment and evacuation procedures in every commercial building.

  • Fire extinguisher (ABC-rated) — One per floor within 75 feet of travel distance. Place near exits and in high-risk areas like kitchens and server rooms. Train employees on the PASS technique.
  • Smoke detectors and alarms — Test monthly and replace batteries annually. Ensure alarms are audible in every room and hallway.
  • Evacuation maps posted — Post maps near elevators, stairwells, and main entrances. Mark primary and secondary exit routes, fire extinguisher locations, and assembly points.
  • Emergency exit signs (illuminated) — Verify all exit signs are lit and visible. Replace burned-out bulbs immediately.
  • Stairwell lighting (battery backup) — Battery-powered应急 lights in stairwells ensure safe evacuation during power outages.
  • Fire-resistant document safe — Store critical documents like insurance policies, employee records, and building plans in a fireproof safe.
  • Evacuation chairs (multi-story buildings) — For employees who cannot use stairs. Place one at each stairwell entrance on upper floors.
  • Assembly point signage — Clear signage at the designated outdoor meeting point where employees gather after evacuation.

Category 2: First Aid and Medical

Workplace injuries range from minor cuts to life-threatening events. A well-stocked first aid station and trained responders save lives.

  • First aid kit (OSHA-compliant) — Include bandages, gauze, antiseptic wipes, medical tape, scissors, tweezers, pain relievers, burn cream, and disposable gloves. Restock after every use.
  • AED (Automated External Defibrillator) — Required in many states for buildings over a certain occupancy. Place in a visible, accessible location. Train at least two employees on use.
  • EpiPen and allergy medication — For offices with employees who have severe allergies. Store in the first aid station with clear labeling.
  • Eye wash station — Required near laboratories, workshops, or areas where chemicals are used. Test monthly.
  • Bloodborne pathogen kit — Include gloves, face shields, biohazard bags, and cleanup supplies for blood or bodily fluid exposure.
  • Trained first aid responders — At least two employees per shift should hold current CPR and first aid certification.
  • Emergency contact list (posted) — Post phone numbers for 911, building security, poison control, and nearby hospitals in break rooms and restrooms.
  • Incident report forms — Keep blank forms in the first aid station for documenting injuries and near-misses.

Category 3: Power Outage and Communication

When the power goes out or communication systems fail, your office needs backup systems to maintain safety and contact emergency services.

  • Battery-powered emergency lights — Automatic emergency lights activate when power fails. Place in hallways, stairwells, and near exits.
  • Flashlights and headlamps — One flashlight per major work area. LED flashlights with fresh batteries stored nearby.
  • Portable power banks — Keep charged power banks available for charging phones during extended outages.
  • Battery-powered or hand-crank radio — Receive emergency broadcasts when internet and cell networks are disrupted.
  • Printed emergency contact list — Do not rely solely on digital contacts. Print phone numbers for all employees, emergency services, and building management.
  • Backup phone charger (car adapter) — If the office has a parking garage, keep car adapters available for charging devices.
  • Emergency PA system or air horn — For buildings without automated alarm systems. An air horn or megaphone can alert an entire floor.
  • Two-way radios — For floor wardens and safety teams. Cell networks may be overloaded during large-scale emergencies.

Category 4: Shelter-in-Place Supplies

During chemical spills, severe weather, or security threats, employees may need to shelter in place for hours. These supplies keep everyone safe and comfortable.

  • Water (1 liter per person) — Store at least one liter per employee for a minimum 24-hour shelter-in-place scenario. Rotate stock every six months.
  • Non-perishable snacks — Energy bars, granola bars, crackers, and dried fruit. Stock enough for every employee for one full day.
  • Plastic sheeting and duct tape — Seal windows, doors, and ventilation during chemical or biological emergencies. Designate one interior room as the shelter room.
  • Dust masks (N95 or P100) — One per employee. Essential during fires, chemical spills, or dust events.
  • Portable toilet supplies — A 5-gallon bucket with a snap lid, toilet bags, and hand sanitizer for extended shelter-in-place scenarios.
  • Battery-powered fan — Provides airflow in sealed rooms during summer shelter-in-place events.
  • Comfort supplies — Blankets, extra water, and basic comfort items reduce stress during extended shelter-in-place periods.
  • Building HVAC shutdown instructions — Post clear instructions for shutting off the ventilation system during chemical emergencies.

Pro Tip

Designate a shelter-in-place coordinator for each floor. This person is responsible for sealing the room, accounting for employees, and communicating with building management. Practice shelter-in-place drills at least once per year.

Category 5: Employee Personal Go-Bags

Encourage every employee to keep a small personal emergency kit at their desk. During an evacuation, these bags provide essentials for the first hours away from the office.

  • Small flashlight or keychain light — Compact and always accessible. LED keychain lights weigh almost nothing.
  • Water bottle and energy bar — A personal water supply and one high-calorie snack for immediate hydration and energy.
  • Phone charger (portable) — A small power bank keeps your phone charged for communication and GPS navigation.
  • Dust mask — A personal N95 mask for each employee in case of smoke, dust, or chemical exposure.
  • Emergency contact card — A laminated card with your phone number, emergency contacts, and any medical information.
  • Cash ($20-$50) — Small bills for emergency transportation, food, or supplies if you cannot return to the office.
  • Basic medications — Personal prescription medications, pain relievers, and any allergy medication.
  • Comfort item — A small item that provides comfort during stressful situations, such as a stress ball, earbuds, or a book.

Category 6: Documentation and Emergency Planning

Paperwork and planning are the backbone of workplace safety. These documents and procedures ensure everyone knows what to do.

  • Written emergency action plan — A documented plan covering evacuation routes, shelter-in-place procedures, medical emergencies, and communication protocols.
  • Employee contact list (updated monthly) — Include home phones, cell phones, and emergency contacts for every employee.
  • Floor warden assignments — Designate at least one warden per floor or department to guide evacuations and account for employees.
  • Building floor plans with exits marked — Keep copies in the emergency kit and post in common areas.
  • Insurance and incident documentation — Store copies of workers compensation insurance, building insurance, and incident report templates.
  • Evacuation drill schedule — Conduct drills at least twice per year. Document dates, participants, and improvement areas.
  • Emergency supply inventory log — Track what supplies you have, when they were purchased, and when they expire.
  • Vendor contact list — Phone numbers for fire extinguisher servicing, first aid restocking, AED maintenance, and building repairs.

Common Office Emergency Preparedness Mistakes

Avoid these frequent mistakes that leave offices vulnerable during emergencies.

  • Skipping fire extinguisher training — Having extinguishers is not enough. Employees must know how and when to use them. Schedule annual hands-on training.
  • Ignoring expired supplies — First aid kits, water, and food expire. Set calendar reminders to check supplies every six months.
  • Blocking emergency exits — Storage boxes, furniture, and equipment block exits in many offices. Conduct monthly exit audits.
  • No designated assembly point — Without a clear meeting point, evacuations become chaotic. Designate and communicate the assembly point to all employees.
  • Relying solely on digital contacts — If your phone dies or the network goes down, you cannot access digital contact lists. Always keep printed copies.
  • Forgetting remote employees — Remote workers need their own emergency supplies and a communication plan that includes them in office emergencies.

Office Emergency Kit Maintenance Schedule

An emergency kit is only useful if everything inside it works when you need it. Follow this maintenance schedule.

  • Monthly — Test fire extinguishers, smoke detectors, and emergency lighting. Verify first aid kits are fully stocked. Check that evacuation maps are posted and current.
  • Every 6 months — Replace expired food, water, and batteries. Update employee contact lists. Review and update the emergency action plan.
  • Annually — Conduct fire extinguisher training for all employees. Service AED units. Replace expired first aid supplies. Review insurance and incident documentation.
  • After every drill or incident — Restock any used supplies. Document lessons learned and update procedures. debrief with floor wardens.
