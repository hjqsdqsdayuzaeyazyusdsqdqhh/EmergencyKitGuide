Roadside Emergency Preparedness: Stay Safe on the Road

A complete guide to roadside emergency preparedness. Learn exactly what to do when your car breaks down, how to protect yourself from traffic, and which supplies can save your life.

Immediate Steps After a Breakdown

When your vehicle breaks down, the first few minutes determine whether you are safe or in danger. Follow this step-by-step protocol every time, regardless of the situation.

Step 1: Activate Hazard Lights

Your hazard lights should be the first thing you turn on. This signals to other drivers that your vehicle is disabled and helps prevent rear-end collisions. Even in daylight, hazard lights significantly increase your vehicle's visibility. If your electrical system has failed and the hazards do not work, turn on your headlights or interior dome light if possible.

Step 2: Pull Over Safely

Steer your vehicle as far to the right as possible. Aim for a wide shoulder, a parking lot, or a side street. Avoid stopping on a curve where approaching drivers cannot see you until the last moment. If you cannot make it to the shoulder, try to keep your vehicle on the pavement rather than on the grass or soft shoulder, which can cause you to slide further into traffic.

Step 3: Turn Off the Engine

Once stopped, turn off the engine completely. If you are stopped on a hill, engage the parking brake and turn your wheels away from the road. This prevents your vehicle from rolling into traffic if the parking brake fails. Leave your hazard lights on.

Step 4: Assess the Situation

Before exiting the vehicle, check your mirrors and look for oncoming traffic. Determine whether it is safer to stay inside or step out. If you are on a busy highway with no shoulder, staying inside with your seat belt on is often the safest option until professional help arrives.

Key Takeaway

Your safety always comes before your vehicle. Do not risk your life trying to fix a car in active traffic. A car can be replaced; you cannot. If in doubt, stay inside, keep your seat belt on, and call for professional help.

Staying Safe on the Roadside

Being stopped on the side of the road puts you in one of the most dangerous positions a civilian can occupy. In the United States, approximately 350 people are killed each year while standing or walking near disabled vehicles on highways. Understanding how to protect yourself is essential.

Visibility is Your Best Defense

The most critical factor in roadside safety is being seen by other drivers. Place reflective warning triangles or LED flares behind your vehicle at increasing distances. On highways, place the first triangle at 10 feet, the second at 100 feet, and the third at 200 feet behind your car. This gives approaching drivers time to see your situation and move over.

The Move Over Law

All 50 U.S. states have Move Over laws that require drivers to change lanes or slow down when approaching stopped emergency vehicles, tow trucks, and in many states, disabled civilian vehicles. However, not all drivers obey these laws. Never assume approaching traffic will move over for you. Take active steps to make yourself visible and stay as far from traffic as possible.

When to Stay Inside vs. Exit

Stay inside if: you are on a busy highway with no shoulder, traffic is heavy and moving fast, it is dark and visibility is poor, or you feel unsafe exiting for any reason. Keep your seat belt on and wait for professional assistance.

Exit the vehicle if: you are on a narrow shoulder with traffic passing close to your car, your vehicle is leaking fuel, there is a fire risk, or you can safely move to a location well away from traffic. Always exit from the side away from traffic.

  • Wear a high-visibility vest when outside your vehicle
  • Stay behind the guardrail whenever one is available
  • Face oncoming traffic so you can see approaching vehicles
  • Keep children and pets inside the vehicle with doors locked
  • Never stand behind or between vehicles involved in a breakdown or accident

Essential Emergency Supplies

The right supplies in your trunk transform a dangerous situation into a manageable one. Here are the most important items for roadside safety.

Communication and Signaling

A fully charged cell phone is your primary lifeline. Keep a portable power bank in your kit so you can charge your phone even if your car's battery is dead. Write emergency numbers on a physical card—do not rely solely on your phone's contact list. Include your insurance roadside assistance number, local towing services, and an out-of-area emergency contact.

Visibility and Warning

Reflective triangles, LED road flares, and a high-visibility vest are the trifecta of roadside visibility. LED flares are reusable, brighter, and work in all weather conditions compared to traditional chemical flares. A windshield sun shade can also be draped over your rear window as an improvised signal in daylight.

Basic Repair Tools

A portable jump starter handles dead batteries without needing another vehicle. A 12V air compressor can reinflate a low tire long enough to reach a service station. A tire sealant spray provides a temporary fix for punctures. A basic tool kit with wrenches, screwdrivers, and pliers handles minor mechanical issues. Common Roadside Emergencies and How to Handle Them

Knowing how to respond to specific emergencies reduces panic and speeds up resolution. Here are the most frequent situations drivers face.

Flat Tire

Pull over to a flat, stable surface away from traffic. Turn on hazard lights and place reflective triangles. If you have a spare tire and the tools to change it, and you feel confident doing so, proceed carefully. If the road is too narrow, traffic is heavy, or conditions are unsafe, call for roadside assistance. A tire sealant can provide a temporary fix to get you to the nearest service station.

Dead Battery

A portable jump starter is the fastest solution. Connect it according to the manufacturer's instructions and start your vehicle. If you need a jump from another driver, make sure both vehicles are in park or neutral with engines off before connecting cables. Connect positive to positive, negative to negative, and start the working vehicle first. For more details, see our dead car battery emergency guide.

Overheating Engine

Turn off the air conditioning and turn the heater on full blast to draw heat away from the engine. Pull over immediately and turn off the engine. Wait at least 30 minutes for the engine to cool before checking the coolant level. Never open the radiator cap on a hot engine—pressurized coolant can cause severe burns. If you see steam, stay away from the hood and call for a tow.

Running Out of Fuel

If your fuel light comes on, find the nearest gas station immediately. Do not risk running out of fuel on a highway. If you do run out, activate hazard lights and pull to the shoulder. Call roadside assistance or a friend to bring fuel. Keep a small fuel container in your trunk for emergencies, but never store gasoline in an enclosed vehicle cabin.

Locked Out

Keep a spare key in your wallet or with a trusted person. Some modern key fobs have a hidden physical key inside that can unlock the driver's door. Roadside assistance services can also unlock most vehicles. Avoid attempting to unlock the car yourself with improvised tools, as this can damage the door or lock mechanism.

Emergency Contact Card

Create a laminated card with your emergency numbers and keep it in your glove box and wallet. Include: 911, your insurance roadside assistance number, a national tow service, your emergency contact, and your vehicle information (year, make, model, VIN, and license plate).

Prevention and Maintenance

The best roadside emergency is the one that never happens. Regular vehicle maintenance dramatically reduces the risk of breakdowns. Follow these guidelines to keep your vehicle reliable.

Monthly Checks

Check your tire pressure including the spare, verify all lights are working, top off windshield washer fluid, and glance under the car for any leaks. These two-minute checks catch problems before they leave you stranded. Learn more about comprehensive vehicle safety in our car safety essentials guide.

Seasonal Maintenance

Before winter: Test your battery, check antifreeze levels, inspect wiper blades, verify your heater and defroster work, and ensure your tires have adequate tread for snow.

Before summer: Check the cooling system, inspect the AC, verify tire pressure (heat increases pressure), and replace worn wiper blades.

Follow Your Service Schedule

Your vehicle's manufacturer provides a maintenance schedule in the owner's manual. Follow it. Oil changes, brake inspections, belt replacements, and fluid flushes are not optional—they are designed to prevent the breakdowns that leave you on the side of the road. Our winter car emergency kit guide covers cold-weather specific maintenance.

  • Oil and filter changes — Every 5,000 to 7,500 miles or per manufacturer recommendation
  • Tire rotation and alignment — Every 6,000 to 8,000 miles
  • Battery testing — Twice per year, before winter and before summer
  • Brake inspection — Every 12,000 miles or when you notice squealing or pulling
  • Belt and hose inspection — Annually, or at every oil change after 60,000 miles

Roadside Emergency FAQs

Only exit your vehicle if it is in a safe location away from traffic. On busy highways, it is often safer to stay inside with your seat belt on and wait for professional assistance. If you must exit, do so from the side away from traffic, wear a high-visibility vest, and stay well behind the guardrail.
Every driver should have saved: 911 for life-threatening emergencies, their auto insurance roadside assistance number, a national roadside assistance service like AAA, their car manufacturer's roadside assistance line, and a trusted local mechanic or towing service. Keep a printed card with these numbers in your wallet and glove box.

Check your tire pressure monthly, monitor fluid levels, and address warning lights immediately. Keeping your battery tested twice yearly also prevents unexpected failures.
