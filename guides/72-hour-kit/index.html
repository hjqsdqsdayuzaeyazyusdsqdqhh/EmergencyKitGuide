72-Hour Survival Kit: Build Yours Step by Step

A complete guide to assembling a 72-hour survival kit that keeps you and your family safe during emergencies. From water and food to shelter and communication, this step-by-step guide covers every essential category.

What Is a 72-Hour Kit?

A 72-hour kit, also known as a three-day emergency kit or go bag, is a portable collection of essential supplies designed to sustain you and your family for at least three days following a disaster or emergency. Whether you are dealing with a natural disaster, power outage, or forced evacuation, having a pre-packed kit ready to go can be the difference between safety and crisis.

The concept is straightforward: emergencies often disrupt normal supply chains, utilities, and transportation for days at a time. A well-prepared 72-hour kit bridges that gap, giving you the food, water, shelter, and tools you need to stay safe while services are restored or while you evacuate to a safer location.

Key Takeaway

A 72-hour kit is your personal safety net. It is not a luxury—it is a necessity that every household should have prepared and ready at all times. FEMA, the Red Cross, and emergency management agencies worldwide recommend every family maintain one.

Why 72 Hours?

The 72-hour timeframe is not arbitrary. It is based on decades of emergency management research and real-world disaster response data. Here is why three days is the critical window:

FEMA's Recommendation

The Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA) recommends that every American household be prepared to be self-sufficient for at least 72 hours following a disaster. This is because emergency services, supply chains, and infrastructure repairs typically take a minimum of three days to reach affected areas after a large-scale event.

Statistical Reality

According to FEMA and the National Emergency Management Association, the average time for emergency services to reach victims after a major disaster is between 48 and 72 hours. In remote or severely impacted areas, this window can extend even longer. During Hurricane Katrina, some residents waited five days or more for assistance. After the 2021 Texas winter storm, many households went without power or water for over a week.

  • Infrastructure recovery — Power grids, water treatment plants, and communication networks typically require 72 hours or more to restore after major events.
  • Supply chain disruption — Grocery stores, pharmacies, and gas stations may be closed or emptied within hours of a disaster announcement.
  • Transportation delays — Road closures, fuel shortages, and traffic congestion can delay evacuation and rescue operations significantly.
  • Communication blackouts — Cell towers and internet infrastructure often fail during severe weather, making it difficult to call for help or access information.

The first 72 hours after a disaster are the most critical. Families who are prepared to handle this window independently are significantly more likely to emerge from emergencies safely and with minimal additional hardship.

Essential Items

A complete 72-hour kit is organized into six core categories. Each category addresses a fundamental survival need. Below is a detailed breakdown of what to include in each.

Water & Food

Water is the single most critical survival supply. The human body can survive only three days without water in most conditions. Food is secondary to water but essential for maintaining energy and morale over a 72-hour period.

  • Water — Store at least one gallon per person per day. For a family of four, that means 12 gallons minimum. Include a portable water filter or purification tablets as a backup.
  • Non-perishable food — Pack enough for three days per person. Choose calorie-dense, shelf-stable foods that require little or no cooking.
  • Manual can opener — If you pack canned goods, a manual can opener is essential. Electric openers are useless without power.
  • Disposable plates and utensils — Without running water, disposable tableware reduces waste and simplifies cleanup.
  • Electrolyte packets — These help prevent dehydration, especially in hot weather or during physical exertion.

Shelter & Warmth

Exposure to the elements is one of the leading causes of emergency-related injuries and deaths. Your kit must include items that protect you from cold, heat, rain, and wind.

  • Emergency tent or tarp — A lightweight emergency shelter provides protection from rain, wind, and sun. Mylar tents are compact and effective.
  • Sleeping bag or emergency blankets — Mylar space blankets retain body heat and are extremely lightweight. For colder climates, a-rated sleeping bag provides better insulation.
  • Rain poncho — A lightweight, packable rain poncho keeps you dry during evacuation or outdoor shelter.
  • Extra clothing layers — Pack moisture-wicking base layers, a warm mid-layer, and a waterproof outer layer. Include extra socks and underwear.
  • Sturdy footwear — Keep a pair of closed-toe shoes or boots accessible in case you need to evacuate barefoot or in inadequate footwear.

First Aid

Injuries are common during and after emergencies. A well-stocked first aid kit allows you to treat minor injuries immediately, preventing them from becoming serious medical issues.

  • Comprehensive first aid kit — Include adhesive bandages, gauze pads, medical tape, antiseptic wipes, antibiotic ointment, tweezers, scissors, and pain relievers.
  • Prescription medications — Maintain a 7-day supply of any essential prescription medications in a waterproof container.
  • Burn treatment supplies — Burn gel, burn cream, and non-stick dressings for treating thermal injuries.
  • Medical information cards — Write down allergies, blood types, medications, and emergency contacts for each family member.

Tools & Supplies

The right tools give you the ability to handle unexpected challenges, from minor repairs to emergency signaling.

  • Multi-tool or Swiss Army knife — A versatile tool with pliers, knife, screwdrivers, and other implements handles dozens of emergency tasks.
  • Flashlight with extra batteries — A high-quality LED flashlight is indispensable. Pack spare batteries in a waterproof bag.
  • Whistle — A loud whistle can signal for help over long distances when your phone is dead or you are trapped.

  • Work gloves — Protect your hands from debris, broken glass, and sharp objects during cleanup or evacuation.
  • Fire-starting materials — Waterproof matches, a lighter, and a fire starter can provide warmth and a signaling method in survival situations.

Communication

Staying informed and being able to call for help are critical during emergencies. When cell towers fail, alternative communication methods become lifesavers.

  • NOAA weather radio — A battery-powered or hand-crank weather radio receives emergency broadcasts even when cell networks are down.
  • Portable phone charger — A fully charged power bank with at least 10,000mAh capacity keeps your phone alive for emergency calls.
  • Emergency contact list — Write key phone numbers on paper. Do not rely solely on your phone's contact list.
  • Local area maps — Paper maps of your region and potential evacuation routes in case GPS and data services are unavailable.

Documents & Money

In an evacuation, you may not have time to gather important documents. Keep copies in your kit at all times.

  • Waterproof document bag — Store copies of IDs, insurance policies, birth certificates, medical records, and bank account information in a sealed, waterproof container.
  • Cash in small bills — ATMs and card readers may be offline. Keep $100 to $200 in small denominations for emergency purchases.
  • USB drive with digital copies — Store digital scans of all important documents on a encrypted USB drive.
  • Emergency meeting point information — Write down your family's designated meeting point and out-of-area emergency contact.

Building Your Kit

Building a 72-hour kit does not need to happen all at once. Follow this step-by-step process to assemble a complete kit over time, starting with the absolute essentials and building toward full preparedness.

Step 1: Choose the Right Container

Start with a durable, portable container. A large backpack, rolling duffel bag, or plastic storage tote all work well. The container should be easy to carry, weather-resistant, and large enough to hold all your supplies without being unwieldy. Avoid cardboard boxes or flimsy bags that will not survive rough handling.

Step 2: Pack Water First

Water is the most important item in your kit and the heaviest. Pack commercial bottled water in sealed containers. If portability is a concern, include a compact water filter (such as a Sawyer Mini or LifeStraw) and water purification tablets as a lightweight backup. Replace stored water every six months.

Step 3: Add Non-Perishable Food

Choose calorie-dense, shelf-stable foods that require no refrigeration and minimal or no cooking. Energy bars, dried fruit, nuts, peanut butter crackers, and freeze-dried meals are excellent choices. Pack enough for three days per person, plus a small surplus. Rotate food items every six months to maintain freshness.

Step 4: Include Shelter and Warmth

Add emergency blankets, a compact tent or tarp, a rain poncho, and extra clothing layers. If you live in a cold climate, prioritize a sleeping bag rated for your region's lowest temperatures. For hot climates, include a lightweight shelter with UV protection and extra water purification supplies.

Step 5: Stock First Aid and Medications

Purchase a comprehensive first aid kit or assemble your own. Include bandages, antiseptics, pain relievers, burn treatment supplies, and any personal prescription medications. Write medical information cards for each family member and store them in the kit.

Step 6: Add Tools and Communication Gear

Pack a flashlight with spare batteries, a multi-tool, a whistle, duct tape, work gloves, and fire-starting materials. Add a NOAA weather radio, a portable phone charger, paper maps, and an emergency contact list. Test all electronics before storing them.

Step 7: Store Documents and Cash

Place copies of identification, insurance, medical records, and important financial documents in a waterproof bag. Add $100 to $200 in small bills and a USB drive with digital document backups. Keep the document bag in an easily accessible location within your kit.

Step 8: Review and Customize

Walk through your kit and identify any gaps. Consider your family's specific needs: infant supplies, pet food, mobility aids, glasses, and any other personal items. Customize the kit for your climate and the specific disasters most likely in your region.

Maintenance Schedule

A 72-hour kit is only useful if everything in it is in working order. Follow this maintenance schedule to ensure your kit remains ready at all times.

Bi-Annual Review (Every 6 Months)

Check expiration dates on all food and water. Replace expired items. Test flashlights and replace batteries. Inspect first aid supplies and restock used or expired items. Update prescription medications. Rotate seasonal clothing.

Monthly Quick Checks

  • Verify your kit is in its designated, accessible location.
  • Confirm the container is intact and free of moisture or damage.
  • Check that your portable phone charger is charged.
  • Verify your NOAA weather radio has fresh batteries.

Annual Deep Review

  • Conduct a full inventory of every item in the kit.
  • Update all documents and contact information.
  • Review and update your family emergency plan.
  • Practice an evacuation drill with all family members.
  • Consider upgrading gear based on lessons learned.

Seasonal Adjustments

  • Spring/Fall: Replace warm-weather and cold-weather clothing as seasons change. Check water stores for freshness.
  • Summer: Add extra sunscreen, insect repellent, and additional water. Consider a battery-powered fan.
  • Winter: Add hand warmers, extra blankets, and cold-weather gear. Ensure your vehicle kit is also updated.

72-Hour Kit FAQs

A basic 72-hour kit costs between $75 and $150 depending on quality and whether you buy pre-made or assemble it yourself. Budget-friendly options exist by purchasing items individually at discount stores. Premium kits with higher-quality gear can run $200 to $400. The most important thing is to have a kit, regardless of cost. Start with the basics and upgrade over time.
Store your 72-hour kit in an easily accessible location near your home's main exit. A garage, closet near the front door, or mudroom are ideal spots. Keep it in a waterproof, durable container such as a duffel bag, plastic tote, or dedicated emergency backpack. Avoid storing it in areas prone to flooding, extreme heat, or where it could be buried under clutter. Every family member should know where the kit is stored.
